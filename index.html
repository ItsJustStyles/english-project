Japan

The Art of Simple Living

Japan is a country with a rich history and a unique way of living. People in Japan live in both modern apartments and traditional wooden houses called machiya or minka. These homes are known for their elegant simplicity and natural materials.

Traditional Japanese homes use sliding doors called shoji in order to divide spaces and let natural light in. Living in a Japanese home teaches you the value of minimalism. If you visit a traditional Japanese house, you will notice that shoes are removed at the entrance — this shows respect for the space.

Japanese gardens are placed next to living areas to bring peace and beauty to daily life. If you want to experience true Japanese culture, visiting a machiya in Kyoto is a wonderful idea.

Stage 2 — Passive Voice & Second Conditional

Traditional Japanese homes are built with wood, paper, and bamboo. Shoji screens are used to let soft light into the rooms. Many of these homes are preserved today as cultural heritage sites. If I lived in a traditional Japanese home, I would wake up every morning to the sound of birds in the garden. If more people understood Japanese architecture, they would appreciate the beauty of simple design.
CN

China

From Courtyard Homes to Earthen Fortresses

China is one of the largest countries in the world, and its homes reflect its many different cultures and climates. Two of the most interesting traditional homes are the hutong (courtyard house) in Beijing and the tulou (earthen building) in Fujian province.

Living in a hutong means sharing space and daily life with your neighbors. If you walk through the hutongs of Beijing, you will feel like you are traveling back in time. People use these spaces to cook, to talk, and to spend time together in order to maintain their community bonds.

The tulou is a large circular building made of earth. If you see a tulou for the first time, you will be amazed by its size and strength. Visiting a tulou helps you understand how communities worked together for safety.

Stage 2 — Passive Voice & Second Conditional

Hutong communities are found in the historic center of Beijing. The tulou buildings were constructed by the Hakka people hundreds of years ago. Today, these structures are recognized as UNESCO World Heritage Sites. The walls are made of earth, wood, and stone. If I lived in a tulou, I would never feel alone because hundreds of people share the same building. If the hutongs were not protected, Beijing would lose an important part of its cultural identity.
MN

Mongolia

Life in a Ger

Mongolia is a vast country with wide open grasslands called steppes. The traditional home of Mongolian people is called a ger (also known as a yurt). A ger is a round, portable tent made of wood and felt — perfect for a nomadic lifestyle.

Living in a ger is a special experience. The ger is easy to assemble and to disassemble, which allows families to move with their animals across the land. If you visit a Mongolian family, you will be welcomed inside their ger with tea and food.

The ger has a hole in the center of the roof in order to let smoke out and light in. Families use colorful carpets and hand-painted patterns to make their homes beautiful. If you look inside a ger, you will see how much meaning and art Mongolian people put into their homes.

Stage 2 — Passive Voice & Second Conditional

The ger is made of a wooden frame covered with thick felt. It is assembled by the whole family in less than two hours. The interior is decorated with traditional patterns that represent nature and the cosmos. Gers have been used by Mongolian nomads for thousands of years. If I lived in a ger, I would sleep under the stars through the roof opening every night. If Mongolia were a warmer country, people would not need the thick felt walls to protect them from the cold.
